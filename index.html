Global Exploratory Data Analysis

Before we narrowed down our scope, we conducted a data analysis to better visualize factors affecting wildifres globally. This was how we found out about the need to localize our research study for a better designed quantitative study. The data was obtained from Global Forest Watch.




Temperature Change from 1999-2022

This is a heatmap generated using Tableau and each block represents the average temperature in a country for that year. The figure shows that noticeable increase in temperature began around 2014-2015. This sparked our interest about whether we might see a similar trend, i.e, a shart increase in forest fires as global temperature rise becomes more severe.




Tree Cover Loss & Burned Area from 1992-2022

We plotted an area map using Tableau and as seen in the figure, there was a sharp increase in burned area around 2013. Burned area continued to remain high, indicating the overall increase in wildfires from around 2013 until 2022 which is where our dataset ended. This indicates that there is a similar increase in wildfires that almost overlaps with temperature rise, but further analysis should be done before a conclusion is made.




Countries Affected by Wildfires

This is a density map generated using Tableau and the color density represents least to most total burned areas from 1999-2022. The map revealed that the country that suffered the most in the last 20 years from wildfires was South Sudan with a total burned area of 2,510,595 hectares.

Exploratory Data Analysis on US wildfires

Although South Sudan saw severe wildfire damage, we decided to focus instead on the United States. This was mainly due to our familiarity with the region, the data availability, and the recent fog that happend in the US which was what sparked our interest in the topic initially. The dataset was a combined, meta-dataset that we obtained from Kaggle.




Total Burned Area from 2011-2015

The dataset contained more than 1.8 million record, so we chose to narrowed our dataset down to fires that occurred between 2011-2015. The figure shows density and severity of forest fires, ranging from least acres burned to most acres burned in each state.




Number of Fires in Each Fire Size Class

In order to get a better insight of our data before we experimented with machine learning, we visualized the number of entries in each fire size class to see if there are any imbalances in our data which would be used for the training set. The dataset is quite imbalanced, which was what prompted us to think of ways to handle the data before we use it to train our model.




Fire Frequency for Each Year

We generated a bar graph to look at the number of entries, grouped by year. There seemed to be a fair number of entries for each year which we thought made this a suitable dataset to be used for our prediction model.

Our Prediction Model

We aimed to gain insights about major wildfires that occurred within the United States from 2005 to 2015. The Kaggle dataset was merged with another dataset documenting annual average temperatures for each state. After data cleaning, the final dataset for machine learning had 823,514 observations.



We selected six explanatory variables, including "DISCOVERY_DOY" (day of the year when the fire was discovered), "FIRE_YEAR" (year of the wildfire), "LATITUDE," "LONGITUDE" (coordinates of wildfire occurrences), "STAT_CAUSE_CODE" (wildfire cause), "STATE" (state where the wildfire occurred), and the annual average temperature for the state as explanatory variables. The target variable was the "wildfire size class" with seven classes (from A to G).



After data preprocessing, one-hot encoding was applied to categorical variables, numeric variables were standardized, and Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique (SMOTE) was used to handle imbalanced data. The dataset was then split into training (70%) and testing (30%) data.



To build a powerful classification model, we tried several classification models, including Random Forest, Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, and XGBoost. They evaluated and compared the models using accuracy and precision. Accuracy measures the proportion of correctly predicted wildfire size classes across all categories, while precision focuses on the correct prediction of large wildfire size classes (D, E, F, and G).

Feature Importance

Feature importances were analyzed, and 14 of the top 15 features were from the "STATE" variable category, indicating the importance of the state in accurately predicting wildfire size classes. Lightning (STAT_CAUSE_CODE = 1) was also a significant predictor.




Overall, XGBoost demonstrated exceptional performance, making it the best model for accurately predicting wildfire size classes. We believe our model can provide valuable insights into wildfire management and preparedness, helping allocate resources to prevent or fight fires effectively. The ROC curves and AUC scores also indicated the model's excellent discrimination ability and predictive performance.
